Providential Mathematics Olympiad

The Basics

Open to anyone who loves math, especially if you want a challenge. Sign up here. The competition is planned to take place from July-September.

Format

  • Round 1: timed round

    • Division I, II, III: 1, 2, 3 hours respectively

  • Round 2: video solution
  • Round 3: open discussion

    • Division I: seminar | Division II: total chaos | Division III: debate

Introduction

The Providential Mathematics Olympiad (PMO) is a challenging olympiad-style mathematics competition which focuses on the development of problem-solving, creative and logical thinking skills. What makes this competition unique is that it requires competitors to communicate their ideas through a variety of formats. We believe these are important skills that aspiring mathematicians should acquire at an early stage. In addition, such experiences are often a valuable learning process that cannot be achieved by solving a lot of problems, hence making it an effective alternative to traditional competition training.

Why Attend

People attend olympiads for different reasons, and it varies from person to person. Sometimes, going through problem sets every day can feel boring and repetitive, and the intense environment of an olympiad can be a great way to recharge. Other times you just want to see how well you've studied, to take on a challenge, to showcase your problem-solving ability, or just to have fun. The goal of the PMO is to encourage the exchange and communication of creative ideas, as well as helping participants think about problems more rigorously and deeply. Even if you don't have these demands, participating won't do you any harm (and it's free!).

The Details

The first two rounds are similar to what you'll find in most major olympiad, except that the second round requires competitors to record a video as a response to a certain task which will be decided by the committee. The third round, on the other hand, is what we hope will be the fun part. Level I participants will engage with other competitors in a Socratic Seminar, where a problem is given and participants take turns "building" a solution. Level II participants will be randomly given a problem they haven't seen before, and compete to see who first gets to the correct answer, after which they will be allowed to discuss freely. Level III participants will debate on a math problem designed by the committee.
